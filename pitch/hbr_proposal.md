# Article Proposal: Harvard Business Review

## The Making of a Thought Leader

*How management science manufactures its own gurus — and what gets lost in the process*

---

## The big idea

Management science has a creation myth. The field likes to believe that thought leaders emerge organically: a scholar does rigorous work, the work gets cited, and the best ideas eventually reach the practitioners who need them. It's a meritocracy story. And like most meritocracy stories, it is roughly half true.
We analyzed 34,341 articles across 19 top management journals, 2.4 million citation links, and the publication records of every scholar who crossed over from academic journals to practitioner outlets between 2005 and 2025. What we found is a system that does not discover thought leaders so much as *manufacture* them — through institutional access, citation networks, and co-authorship pipelines that systematically favor a small cluster of elite schools, regardless of research quality.

This is not a story about bad actors. No one is conspiring in a back room. It is something more interesting: a self-reinforcing system so structurally elegant that the people inside it rarely notice it working, and the people outside it rarely get the chance to say so.

---

## What a thought leader is actually made of

**Step 1: Get the right address.**

Only 7% of management scholars are affiliated with a top-15 business school. Yet these scholars are 4.2x overrepresented among academics who cross over to practitioner outlets like HBR and MIT Sloan Management Review. This is striking on its own — but it could still reflect talent concentration. Elite schools hire well. Perhaps the pipeline is simply reflecting talent. Not exactly.


**Step 2: Benefit from the citation cartel.**

When we mapped who cites whom across the entire network, the pattern is not visible in raw percentages — Tier 1 scholars cite Tier 3 scholars 63% of the time, which looks generous. But Tier 3 scholars are 85% of the population. Adjusted for base rates, elite scholars cite other elite scholars at 2.8x their population share while under-citing non-elite scholars by 25%. The attention is not proportional. It circulates within a prestige loop.

The Gini coefficient of citations in management science is 0.76 — higher than U.S. income inequality. The top 1% of authors capture 21% of all citations. The top 10% capture 65%. The field preaches disruption while practicing concentration.

**Step 3: Survive the quality test — and still lose.**

This is where the meritocracy story breaks down completely.

We compared scholars at the *same citation level* — our proxy for demonstrated research quality. At every level, from modestly cited to highly cited, scholars at elite institutions are 4 to 5 times more likely to cross over to practitioner outlets. A scholar with 2,000+ citations in top journals has a 31% chance of reaching HBR from Harvard. The same scholar, equally cited, has a 6% chance from a non-elite university.

The gap does not close at higher quality. It does not narrow with more publications. It is not a talent problem. It is an access problem wearing a meritocracy costume.

**Step 4: Find a sponsor.**

For those who do try to break in, the entry fee is social capital. 61% of first-time publications in top management journals involve a senior co-author. Among non-elite scholars who manage to cross over to practitioner outlets, 37% co-authored with an elite-institution scholar — nearly three times the base rate. The apprenticeship is not optional. And apprenticeships, by design, reproduce the network they belong to.

**Step 5: Watch the funnel do its work.**

Each stage of the pipeline compounds the institutional advantage. We traced every management bestselling author of the past two decades back through practitioner outlets and academic journals. The concentration at each stage is striking:

| Pipeline stage | % from elite institutions |
|---|---|
| All management academics (n=35,194) | 7% |
| Crossover to HBR / MIT SMR (n=583) | 30% |
| Bestselling management book authors, academic origin (n=32) | 91% |
| Full pipeline: journals + HBR + bestseller (n=12) | 92% |

By the time a management idea reaches a bookshelf at an airport, there is a 91% chance it was produced at one of roughly 15 schools. The 12 scholars who completed the full journey — top academic journals, practitioner outlets, and bestselling books — are, with one exception, at Harvard, Stanford, Wharton, MIT, Chicago, Duke, or London Business School. The names are familiar: Grant, Edmondson, Porter, Christensen, Pfeffer, Ariely, Thaler. The addresses are more familiar still.

The single academic-origin management bestseller from outside the elite tier is Brené Brown, at the University of Houston — and she arguably reached practitioners through a different pipeline entirely (TED, Netflix) rather than through the academic-to-HBR crossover system.

This is not a leaky pipeline. It is a funnel — and it works exactly as designed.

## Why this matters beyond academia

It would be tempting to read this as an academic governance problem — interesting to professors, irrelevant to everyone else. That would be a mistake.

Every executive who reads this publication, hires a strategy consultant, or invites a keynote speaker at a leadership offsite is consuming ideas that passed through the system we just described. The supply chain of management ideas has a bottleneck, and it is shaped like a university crest.

This creates three problems that practitioners should care about:

**The ideas are too narrow.** If the scholars reaching practitioners are disproportionately at 15 institutions in the U.S. and Western Europe, then the research agenda reflects those contexts. Who is studying middle-market firm resilience in emerging economies? Informal leadership structures in family-owned conglomerates? Probably someone whose work will never reach this page. Not because it isn't good enough — but because it was produced at the wrong address.

**The diversity is cosmetic.** Organizations invest in cognitive diversity — hiring leaders with different backgrounds, perspectives, geographies. But if the management frameworks those leaders are trained on all flow through the same institutional pipeline, the diversity is architectural, not intellectual. The monoculture is not in the boardroom. It is in the reading list.

**The quality signal is broken.** "Published in HBR" and "highly cited" function as quality markers when organizations select advisors, board members, and thought partners. Our data suggests these markers capture institutional access as much as they capture insight. They are not false signals, exactly — but they are noisier than anyone would like to admit, and they systematically exclude a class of scholar whose work may be equally or more relevant.

## A note on irony

We are aware of what we are doing. Proposing an article about the gatekeeping of management thought to the most influential gatekeeper of management thought is either audacious or naive, and probably some of both.

But HBR has a track record of publishing work that interrogates the systems it participates in. And we are not arguing that the institution is malicious — we are arguing that it is structural, and that structures can be redesigned. If the field's most visible outlet took this question seriously, it would not just be an article. It would be a signal.

We are also not arguing that elite institutions produce bad research. They obviously attract exceptional scholars. We are arguing something more specific and more uncomfortable: that equally good research, produced elsewhere, is systematically less likely to reach the people who could use it. This is not a scandal. It is a market failure in the ideas economy — and the field that studies market failures ought to be interested in its own.

## Format and structure

We envision this as a **feature article (3,000-4,000 words)**:

1. **The two scholars** — an opening vignette: two management researchers with comparable citation records, comparable h-indices, comparable publication volume. One is a household name among practitioners. The other is virtually unknown outside her subfield. The difference is not talent. (~500 words)

2. **The manufacturing process** — the four-step pipeline above, presented with data visualizations. We have publication-ready charts: a citation Lorenz curve, a tier-to-tier citation heatmap, the quality-controlled crossover analysis, and an institutional concentration map. (~1,500 words)

3. **The mechanism** — how the system sustains itself without anyone steering it. Editorial boards, conference networks, PhD placement pipelines, and the subtle sociology of "fit." (~700 words)

4. **The cost** — what practitioners are missing and why they should care. (~500 words)

5. **What could change** — not a naive call for "more inclusion," but structural interventions: blind editorial processes, practitioner outlet scouting beyond elite networks, citation-aware diversity metrics for journals. (~500 words)

## Methodology

This analysis uses OpenAlex, an open-access bibliometric database, to construct a complete citation network of 34,341 articles published in 19 top management journals (AMJ, AMR, ASQ, SMJ, Organization Science, Management Science, and 13 others) between 2005 and 2025. Institutional tiers are based on the FT Global MBA ranking and historical research output. "Crossover" scholars (n=583) are operationalized as academics who publish in both top-tier academic journals and practitioner outlets (HBR and MIT Sloan Management Review), identified through OpenAlex's source indexing. The quality-controlled analysis compares crossover rates within citation-level bins to isolate institutional advantage from research productivity. All data and code are open-source and reproducible.
